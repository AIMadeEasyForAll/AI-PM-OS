# Skill: Stakeholder Management

Use this skill to communicate scope, tradeoffs, and decisions to people who have influence but aren't in the day-to-day work — execs, sales, support leads, other PMs.

## When to use this

- Writing an executive summary or status update.
- Saying no to a request without burning the relationship.
- A decision needs buy-in from people who weren't in the room when it was made.

## Inputs needed

- The decision or update you need to communicate.
- Who the audience is, and what they care about (revenue, customer happiness, technical risk, their own roadmap).
- Any prior commitment or expectation you're confirming, changing, or walking back.

## Method

1. **Lead with the decision or the ask, not the journey.** First sentence: what you decided, or what you need from them. The backstory, if needed, comes after — most readers stop after sentence two.
2. **Translate to their incentive, not yours.** A sales stakeholder cares about the deal at risk; an exec cares about the metric tied to their goals. Frame the same fact differently per audience rather than sending one message to all.
3. **Separate fact from recommendation from ask.** "Here's what we know / here's what I think we should do / here's what I need from you" as three distinct, labeled parts — don't blend them into a paragraph of prose.
4. **When saying no, offer the tradeoff, not just the rejection.** "Not this quarter, because X is ahead of it for Y reason — revisit in Z" beats a bare no every time.
5. **Close the loop in writing.** Verbal alignment in a hallway isn't a decision until it's written down somewhere stakeholders can reference later, especially the ones who weren't in the hallway.

## Output

A short written update: decision/ask (1-2 sentences), context (3-5 sentences max), what's needed from the reader, and where to follow up with questions.

## Common failure modes

- Writing the update for the work you did, not the decision the reader needs to make. Cut anything that doesn't change what the reader does next.
- Avoiding a hard conversation by burying the bad news in paragraph four. If it's bad news, it goes in sentence one.
- Re-explaining the same context to the same stakeholder every time instead of pointing back to a written decision log — see [`decision-log-template.md`](../artifacts/decision-log-template.md).
